## THE GENERAL'S MAP TABLE

Picture a war room in 1944. General Eisenhower stands over a massive map table planning the D-Day invasion.
Around him, commanders report on different divisions:

\*\*<Highlight variant="yellow">Commander A:</Highlight> "Sir, we advanced 12 kilometers today."

\*\*<Highlight variant="yellow">Commander B:</Highlight> "Sir, we captured 3 villages."

\*\*<Highlight variant="yellow">Commander C:</Highlight> "Sir, we lost 47 men but eliminated 200 enemy soldiers."

Which commander gave Eisenhower <Highlight variant="blue">useful information?</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="yellow">Commander C.</Highlight>**

Because Commander C reported in <Highlight variant="yellow">relative terms</Highlight> — losses versus gains. This ratio tells Eisenhower
whether that division is winning or losing the war.

Commanders A and B reported <Highlight variant="yellow">absolute numbers</Highlight> without context. 12 kilometers sounds impressive —
unless you lost 1,000 men to gain it. 3 villages sounds great — unless they were abandoned and
tactically worthless.

Professional military commanders don't think in absolute territories or casualties.
They think in <Highlight variant="blue">ratios, efficiencies, and strategic value.</Highlight>

Professional traders are no different.

If I tell you "I made $5,000 this month," you have no idea if I'm a successful trader or a failing one.

Did I risk $50,000 to make $5,000? (terrible risk-reward ratio 1:0.1)<br/>
Did I risk $500 to make $5,000? (1000% return — extraordinary)<br/>
Did I make $5,000 but my account dropped from $50,000 to $45,000?
(Actually lost $45,000, made back $5,000 — terrible)
Dollar amounts are meaningless without context.

This is why professional traders invented a new language — <Highlight variant="blue">a measurement system that makes performance comparable, trackable, and meaningful regardless of account size.</Highlight>

This language is built on two concepts:

1\. <Highlight variant="yellow">R-Multiples</Highlight> — The universal unit of trading performance<br/>
2\. <Highlight variant="yellow">Expectancy</Highlight> — The mathematical expression of your edge

## What is an R-Multiple? (THE UNIVERSAL TRADING UNIT)

**<Highlight variant="yellow">R (or 1R) = The amount you risked on a trade</Highlight>**

That's it. That's the entire definition.

If you <Highlight variant="yellow">risked $100 on a trade</Highlight>, then <Highlight variant="blue">1R = $100 for that trade.</Highlight><br/>
If you <Highlight variant="yellow">risked $500 on a trade</Highlight>, then <Highlight variant="blue">1R = $500 for that trade.</Highlight><br/>
If you <Highlight variant="yellow">risked $50 on a trade</Highlight>, then <Highlight variant="blue">1R = $50 for that trade.</Highlight><br/>

**<Highlight variant="red">R is your personal, trade-specific unit of risk.</Highlight>**

## WHY R-MULTIPLES ARE REVOLUTIONARY

Instead of measuring profit/loss in dollars, we measure in R-multiples:

**<Highlight variant="yellow">Example 1:</Highlight>**

→ You risked $100 (1R)<br/>
→ You made $200 profit<br/>
→ <Highlight variant="green">Result: +2R</Highlight> (You made 2× what you risked)<br/>

**<Highlight variant="yellow">Example 2:</Highlight>**

→ You risked $100 (1R)<br/>
→ You lost $100<br/>
→ <Highlight variant="red">Result: -1R</Highlight> (You lost exactly what you risked)<br/>

**<Highlight variant="yellow">Example 3:</Highlight>**

→ You risked $100 (1R)<br/>
→ You made $350 profit<br/>
→ <Highlight variant="green">Result: +3.5R</Highlight> (You made 3.5× what you risked)<br/>

**Do you see the elegance?**

R-multiples tell you how much you made <Highlight variant="blue">relative</Highlight> to what you risked,
**<Highlight variant="red">regardless of account size or dollar amounts.</Highlight>**

## THE R-MULTIPLE FORMULA

<Math>
  {'\\text{R-Multiple} = \\frac{\\text{Profit or Loss}}{\\text{Initial Risk}}'}
</Math>

Or more precisely:

<Math>
  {
    '\\text{R-Multiple} = \\frac{\\text{Exit Price} - \\text{Entry Price}}{\\text{Entry Price} - \\text{Stop Loss Price}}'
  }
</Math>

For <Highlight variant="yellow">long trades:</Highlight>

<Math>
  {
    '\\text{R} = \\frac{\\text{Exit} - \\text{Entry}}{\\text{Entry} - \\text{Stop}}'
  }
</Math>

For <Highlight variant="yellow">short trades:</Highlight>

<Math>
  {
    '\\text{R} = \\frac{\\text{Entry} - \\text{Exit}}{\\text{Stop} - \\text{Entry}}'
  }
</Math>

### <Highlight variant="accent">EXAMPLE: CALCULATING R FOR A LONG TRADE</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Trade setup:</Highlight>**

→ Entry: EUR/USD at 1.1000<br/>
→ Stop loss: 1.0950 (50 pips below)<br/>
→ Exit: 1.1150 (150 pips above)<br/>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Calculate R:</Highlight>**

<Math>
  {'\\begin{aligned}' +
    '\\text{Initial Risk} &= \\text{Entry Price} - \\text{Stop Loss Price} = 50 \\text{ pips} = 1R \\\\' +
    '\\text{Actual Profit} &= \\text{Exit Price} - \\text{Entry Price} = 150 \\text{ pips} \\\\' +
    '\\text{R-Multiple} &= \\frac{\\text{Actual Profit}}{\\text{Initial Risk}} = \\frac{150 \\text{ pips}}{50 \\text{ pips}} = 3R' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>

**<Highlight variant="green">Result: +3R trade</Highlight>**

**<Highlight variant="blue">Translation:</Highlight>** You made 3 times what you risked. If you risked $100, you made $300.

### <Highlight variant="accent">EXAMPLE: CALCULATING R FOR A SHORT TRADE</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Trade setup:</Highlight>**

→ Entry: GBP/JPY short at 185.00<br/>
→ Stop loss: 185.60 (60 pips above)<br/>
→ Exit: 184.20 (80 pips below)<br/>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Calculate R:</Highlight>**

<Math>
  {'\\begin{aligned}' +
    '\\text{Initial Risk} &= \\text{Entry Price} - \\text{Stop Loss Price} = 60 \\text{ pips} = 1R \\\\' +
    '\\text{Actual Profit} &= \\text{Exit Price} - \\text{Entry Price} = 80 \\text{ pips} \\\\' +
    '\\text{R-Multiple} &= \\frac{\\text{Actual Profit}}{\\text{Initial Risk}} = \\frac{80 \\text{ pips}}{60 \\text{ pips}} = 1.33R' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>

**<Highlight variant="green">Result: +1.33R trade</Highlight>**

### <Highlight variant="accent">EXAMPLE: CALCULATING R FOR A LOSING TRADE</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Trade setup:</Highlight>**

→ Entry: Gold at $2,000<br/>
→ Stop loss: $1,990 (risk $10)
→ Stopped out at $1,990

**<Highlight variant="blue">Calculate R:</Highlight>**

<Math>
  {'\\begin{aligned}' +
    '\\text{Initial Risk} &= \\text{Entry Price} - \\text{Stop Loss Price} = 10 \\text{ dollars} = 1R \\\\' +
    '\\text{Actual Loss} &= \\text{Exit Price} - \\text{Entry Price} = 10 \\text{ dollars} \\\\' +
    '\\text{R-Multiple} &= \\frac{\\text{Actual Loss}}{\\text{Initial Risk}} = \\frac{10 \\text{ dollars}}{10 \\text{ dollars}} = -1R' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>

**<Highlight variant="green">Result: -1R trade</Highlight>**

**<Highlight variant="blue">Translation:</Highlight>** This is a perfect loss. You lost exactly what you planned to risk, no more, no less.
Your stop did its job.

### <Highlight variant="accent">EXAMPLE: CALCULATING R FOR A PARTIAL LOSS</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Trade setup:</Highlight>**

→ Entry: AAPL at $180
→ Stop loss: $176 (risk $4)
→ Exited early at $178 (down $2)

**<Highlight variant="blue">Calculate R:</Highlight>**

<Math>
  {'\\begin{aligned}' +
    '\\text{Initial Risk} &= \\text{Entry Price} - \\text{Stop Loss Price} = 4 \\text{ dollars} = 1R \\\\' +
    '\\text{Actual Loss} &= \\text{Exit Price} - \\text{Entry Price} = 2 \\text{ dollars} \\\\' +
    '\\text{R-Multiple} &= \\frac{\\text{Actual Loss}}{\\text{Initial Risk}} = \\frac{2 \\text{ dollars}}{4 \\text{ dollars}} = -0.5R' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>

**<Highlight variant="green">Result: -0.5R trade</Highlight>**

**<Highlight variant="blue">Translation:</Highlight>** You lost half of what you were willing to risk.
Maybe you <Highlight variant="yellow">saw deteriorating conditions</Highlight> and exited early — smart risk management.

### <Highlight variant="accent">WHY R-MULTIPLES ARE SUPERIOR TO DOLLAR AMOUNTS</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Consider three traders:</Highlight>**

**<Highlight variant="yellow">TRADER A:</Highlight>**

→ Account: $10,000<br/>
→ Risks 1% per trade = $100<br/>
→ Makes $200 profit

**<Highlight variant="yellow">TRADER B:</Highlight>**

→ Account: $100,000<br/>
→ Risks 1% per trade = $1,000<br/>
→ Makes $2,000 profit

**<Highlight variant="yellow">TRADER C:</Highlight>**

→ Account: $500,000<br/>
→ Risks 1% per trade = $5,000<br/>
→ Makes $10,000 profit

**<Highlight variant="blue">In dollar terms:</Highlight>**

→ Trader A made $200 (looks weak)<br/>
→ Trader B made $2,000 (looks moderate)<br/>
→ Trader C made $10,000 (looks amazing)

**<Highlight variant="blue">In R-multiples:</Highlight>**

→ Trader A: $200 ÷ $100 = <Highlight variant="green">+2R</Highlight><br/>
→ Trader B: $2,000 ÷ $1,000 = <Highlight variant="green">+2R</Highlight><br/>
→ Trader C: $10,000 ÷ $5,000 = <Highlight variant="green">+2R</Highlight><br/>

**All three traders had identical performance: <Highlight variant="green">+2R</Highlight>.**

**<Highlight variant="blue">R-multiples make performance comparable across all account sizes.</Highlight>**

This is why professional prop firms, hedge funds, and trading educators measure everything in R.

When you talk to professionals, they don't say <Highlight variant="yellow">"I made $50,000 last month."</Highlight>

They say <Highlight variant="yellow">"I made 28R last month."</Highlight>

And every professional immediately understands what that means:<br/>
Exceptional performance, regardless of whether their account is $10,000 or $10 million.

## EXPECTANCY: THE MATHEMATICAL EXPRESSION OF YOUR EDGE

Now we arrive at the single most important metric in trading:

**<Highlight variant='blue'>EXPECTANCY</Highlight>**

Expectancy tells you **how much you expect to make, on average, per trade** over a large sample.

It's the mathematical proof that you have (or don't have) an edge.

### <Highlight variant="accent">THE EXPECTANCY FORMULA</Highlight>

<Math>
  {
    '\\text{Expectancy (in R)} = (\\text{Win Rate} \\times \\text{Avg Win Size}) - (\\text{Loss Rate} \\times \\text{Avg Loss Size})'
  }
</Math>

Or more precisely:

<Math>
  {
    '\\text{Expectancy} = (\\text{\\% Wins} \\times \\text{Avg R of Wins}) - (\\text{\\% Losses} \\times \\text{Avg R of Losses})'
  }
</Math>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Example:</Highlight>**

→ Win rate: 50%<br/>
→ Average win: +2R<br/>
→ Loss rate: 50%<br/>
→ Average loss: -1R<br/>

<Math>
  {'\\begin{aligned}' +
    '\\text{Expectancy} &= (0.50 \\times 2R) - (0.50 \\times 1R) \\\\' +
    '\\text{Expectancy} &= 1.0R - 0.5R \\\\' +
    '\\text{Expectancy} &= +0.5R \\text{ per trade}' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>What this means:</Highlight>**

Over 100 trades, you expect to make 100 × 0.5R = +50R

If you risk 1% per trade, that's +50% return over 100 trades.

### <Highlight variant="accent">CALCULATING YOUR PERSONAL EXPECTANCY</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Let's work through a real example from a journal:</Highlight>**

**<Highlight variant='yellow'>Your last 50 trades:</Highlight>**

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Wins:</Highlight>** 28 trades

→ 10 trades @ +1R = +10R<br/>
→ 12 trades @ +2R = +24R<br/>
→ 4 trades @ +3R = +12R<br/>
→ 2 trades @ +5R = +10R<br/>
→ Total wins: +56R<br/>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Losses:</Highlight>** 22 trades

→ 20 trades @ -1R = -20R<br/>
→ 2 trades @ -1.5R = -3R (stop moved or slippage)<br/>
→ Total losses: -23R

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Calculate Expectancy:</Highlight>**

<Math>
  {'\\begin{aligned}' +
    '\\text{Win rate} &= \\frac{28}{50} = 56\\% \\\\' +
    '\\text{Loss rate} &= \\frac{22}{50} = 44\\% \\\\' +
    '\\text{Average win} &= \\frac{56R}{28} = 2R \\\\' +
    '\\text{Average loss} &= \\frac{-23R}{22} = -1.05R \\\\' +
    '\\text{Expectancy} &= (0.56 \\times 2R) - (0.44 \\times 1.05R) \\\\' +
    '\\text{Expectancy} &= 1.12R - 0.462R \\\\' +
    '\\text{Expectancy} &= +0.658R \\text{ per trade}' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>What this means:</Highlight>**

→ You have a genuine edge<br/>
→ You expect to make 0.658R per trade on average<br/>
→ Over 100 trades, you expect +65.8R<br/>
→ At 1% risk per trade, that's +65.8% return<br/>
→ This is professional-grade performance<br/>

## MAE & MFE: THE ADVANCED METRICS THAT REVEAL HIDDEN TRUTHS

Now we're entering professional territory—metrics that 99% of retail traders have never heard of
but that every institutional trader tracks religiously.

### <Highlight variant="accent">MAE: MAXIMUM ADVERSE EXCURSION</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Definition:</Highlight>** The largest move against you during a trade, before the trade closes.

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Why it matters:</Highlight>** MAE tells you if your stops are too tight or if you're exiting winners too early.

### <Highlight variant="accent">EXAMPLE: MAE ANALYSIS ON A WINNING TRADE</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant='yellow'>Trade:</Highlight>**

→ Entry: 1.1000<br/>
→ Stop: 1.0950 (50 pips, 1R risk)<br/>
→ Exit: 1.1150 (150 pips, +3R win)<br/>

**<Highlight variant='yellow'>But during the trade:</Highlight>**

→ Price dropped to 1.0970 before rallying

**<Highlight variant='blue'>MAE calculation:</Highlight>**

<Math>
  {'\\begin{aligned}' +
    '\\text{MAE} &= \\text{Entry} - \\text{Lowest Point During Trade} \\\\' +
    '\\text{MAE} &= 1.1000 - 1.0970 = 0.3R' +
    '\\text{MAE} &= 0.3R' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>What this tells you:</Highlight>**

→ Your stop at 50 pips (1R) was appropriate — Price came within 0.6R of your stop but didn't hit it<br/>
→ You gave the trade enough room — If your stop was at 0.5R (25 pips),
you would have been stopped out on a +3R winner<br/>
→ Lesson: Don't use stops tighter than 0.6R for this type of setup<br/>

### <Highlight variant="accent">MAE PATTERN ANALYSIS: THE GOLDMINE</Highlight>

Track MAE across 100 trades. You might discover:

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Pattern 1:</Highlight>** Winners rarely go more than 0.5R against you

→ <Highlight variant='yellow'>Insight:</Highlight> Your entries are well-timed
→ <Highlight variant='yellow'>Action:</Highlight> You might be able to use slightly tighter stops (0.7R instead of 1R)

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Pattern 2:</Highlight>** Winners often go 0.8R against you before winning

→ <Highlight variant='yellow'>Insight:</Highlight> Your entries are early or your stops are too tight
→ <Highlight variant='yellow'>Action:</Highlight> Either wait for better entries or use 1.2R stops

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Pattern 3:</Highlight>** All your losers went more than 1.2R against you before being stopped

→ <Highlight variant='yellow'>Insight:</Highlight> You're moving your stops wider when trades go against you
→ <Highlight variant='yellow'>Action:</Highlight> Stop widening stops! Trust your original stop placement

### <Highlight variant="accent">MFE: MAXIMUM FAVORABLE EXCURSION</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Definition:</Highlight>** The largest move in your favor during a trade, before the trade closes.

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Why it matters:</Highlight>** MFE tells you if you're letting winners run or cutting them too early.

### <Highlight variant="accent">EXAMPLE: MFE ANALYSIS ON A LOSING TRADE</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant='yellow'>Trade:</Highlight>**

→ Entry: 1.1000<br/>
→ Stop: 1.0950 (50 pips, 1R risk)<br/>
→ Stopped out at 1.0950 (-1R loss)<br/>

**<Highlight variant='yellow'>But during the trade:</Highlight>**

→ Price rallied to 1.1080 before reversing

**<Highlight variant='blue'>MFE calculation:</Highlight>**

<Math>
  {'\\begin{aligned}' +
    '\\text{MFE} &= \\text{Highest Point} - \\text{Entry} \\\\' +
    '\\text{MFE} &= 1.1080 - 1.1000 = 0.8R' +
    '\\text{MFE} &= 0.8R' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>What this tells you:</Highlight>**

→ You had a +1.6R profit at one point but ended with -1R loss<br/>
→ You didn't use a trailing stop<br/>
→ You let a winning trade become a losing trade<br/>
→ Lesson: Once you're +1R in profit, trail your stop to at least breakeven<br/>

### <Highlight variant="accent">MFE PATTERN ANALYSIS: CAPTURING MORE PROFIT</Highlight>

Track MFE across 100 trades. You might discover:

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Pattern 1:</Highlight>** Your winners hit +2.5R MFE but you exit at +1.8R avg

→ <Highlight variant='yellow'>Insight:</Highlight> You're exiting too early due to fear
→ <Highlight variant='yellow'>Action:</Highlight> Implement trailing stops, let winners run to +2R minimum

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Pattern 2:</Highlight>** Your losers often show +0.8R MFE before reversing

→ <Highlight variant='yellow'>Insight:</Highlight> Your thesis was temporarily right, but you didn't protect profits
→ <Highlight variant='yellow'>Action:</Highlight> Move stop to breakeven once price hits +0.5R to +1R

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Pattern 3:</Highlight>** Your +1R winners often had +3R MFE

→ <Highlight variant='yellow'>Insight:</Highlight> You're leaving massive profits on the table
→ <Highlight variant='yellow'>Action:</Highlight> Use wider targets or much looser trailing stops

### <Highlight variant="accent">MAE/MFE COMBINED ANALYSIS: THE TRUTH MATRIX</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Create a scatter plot of all your trades:</Highlight>**

→ X-axis: MAE (maximum drawdown during trade)<br/>
→ Y-axis: MFE (maximum profit during trade)<br/>
→ Color: Red = Loss, Green = Win

**<Highlight variant='accent'>Patterns you might see:</Highlight>**

**<Highlight variant='blue'>PATTERN A:</Highlight>** "Perfect Execution"

→ Winners: Low MAE (0.3-0.5R), High MFE (2-4R)<br/>
→ Losers: High MAE (0.9-1R), Low MFE (0-0.3R)<br/>
→ Translation: Your stops are perfect, your entries are great, your exits are good

**<Highlight variant='blue'>PATTERN B:</Highlight>** "Stops Too Tight"

→ Winners: Moderate MAE (0.7-0.9R), High MFE (2-4R)<br/>
→ Losers: Low MAE (0.4-0.6R), Moderate MFE (0.5-1R)<br/>
→ Translation: Many losers had winning potential but your stops are too tight

**<Highlight variant='blue'>PATTERN C:</Highlight>** "Early Exits"

→ Winners: Low MAE (0.3-0.5R), Moderate MFE (1.5-2R)<br/>
→ Final R: Only +1R to +1.3R<br/>
→ Translation: You're exiting way before maximum profit—fear-based trading

**<Highlight variant='blue'>PATTERN D:</Highlight>** "Disaster Zone"

→ Losers: High MAE (>1.2R), High MFE (>1R)<br/>
→ Translation: You're moving stops wider AND not taking profits when you should—emotional chaos
