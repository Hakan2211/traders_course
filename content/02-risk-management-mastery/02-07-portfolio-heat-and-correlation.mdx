## WHAT IS PORTFOLIO HEAT? (THE TOTAL EXPOSURE)

Let's start with the foundational concept:

**<Highlight variant='blue'>PORTFOLIO HEAT</Highlight>** = Total capital at risk across ALL open positions

Or more precisely:

<Math>
  {
    '\\text{Portfolio Heat} = \\sum (\\text{Risk per Position} \\times \\text{Number of Positions})'
  }
</Math>
<br />

But it's not that simple when <Highlight variant='yellow'>correlation enters the picture.</Highlight>

Let me show you the difference between <Highlight variant='blue'>theoretical heat</Highlight> and <Highlight variant='blue'>actual heat.</Highlight>

Actual portfolio heat ≈ Theoretical portfolio heat = 4%

## THE PORTFOLIO HEAT RULE: 6-10% MAXIMUM

Professional traders follow a strict rule:

**<Highlight variant='yellow'>NEVER have more than 6-10% of your total capital at risk across ALL positions simultaneously.</Highlight>**

This is your maximum portfolio heat limit.

### <Highlight variant='accent'>WHY 6-10%?</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant='yellow'>At 6% portfolio heat:</Highlight>**

→ If all positions hit stops simultaneously (worst case)<br/>
→ You lose 6% of your account<br/>
→ Recovery needed: +6.4%<br/>
→ Drawdown: Painful but manageable<br/>
→ Psychological impact: Survivable<br/>

**<Highlight variant='yellow'>At 10% portfolio heat:</Highlight>**

→ If all positions hit stops simultaneously<br/>
→ You lose 10% of your account<br/>
→ Recovery needed: +11.1%<br/>
→ Drawdown: Very difficult<br/>
→ Psychological impact: Severe but survivable<br/>

**<Highlight variant='yellow'>At 20% portfolio heat (violation):</Highlight>**

If all positions hit stops simultaneously
You lose 20% of your account
Recovery needed: +25%
Drawdown: Career-threatening
Psychological impact: Potentially terminal

## THE CORRELATION MATRIX: UNVEILING HIDDEN RISK

Now we enter the realm where most retail traders get obliterated:

### <Highlight variant='accent'>CORRELATION</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Correlation</Highlight> = The degree to which two assets move together**

Measured on a scale from <Highlight variant='blue'>-1.0</Highlight> to <Highlight variant='blue'>+1.0</Highlight>:

→ +1.0 = Perfect positive correlation (move in exact same direction)<br/>
→ 0.0 = No correlation (independent movements)<br/>
→ -1.0 = Perfect negative correlation (move in exact opposite directions)<br/>

## HIDDEN CORRELATION: THE USD TRAP

The most common correlation mistake in forex trading:

Not recognizing that you're trading the SAME CURRENCY in multiple pairs.

### <Highlight variant='accent'>UNDERSTANDING FOREX PAIR STRUCTURE</Highlight>

Every forex pair has TWO currencies:

EUR/USD = Euro / US Dollar

→ Going LONG = You're buying EUR, selling USD<br/>
→ Going SHORT = You're selling EUR, buying USD<br/>

GBP/USD = British Pound / US Dollar

→ Going LONG = You're buying GBP, selling USD<br/>
→ Going SHORT = You're selling GBP, buying USD<br/>

### <Highlight variant='accent'>THE HIDDEN EXPOSURE CALCULATOR</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Question:</Highlight>** If you're long EUR/USD, GBP/USD, and AUD/USD (all 1% risk each), what's your actual USD exposure?

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Answer:</Highlight>**

→ Long EUR/USD = Short 1% USD<br/>
→ Long GBP/USD = Short 1% USD<br/>
→ Long AUD/USD = Short 1% USD<br/>
→ Total USD exposure: Short 3% USD<br/>

You have <Highlight variant='yellow'>THREE TIMES the USD exposure you thought.</Highlight>

## IDENTIFYING CORRELATION: THE PRACTICAL METHODS

Let's make this actionable — how do you identify correlation BEFORE it destroys you?

### <Highlight variant='accent'>METHOD 1: THE COMMON CURRENCY TEST</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Rule:</Highlight>** If two forex positions share a common currency, they're likely correlated.

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Examples:</Highlight>**

Positions: Long EUR/USD + Long EUR/GBP<br/>

→ Common currency: EUR<br/>
→ If EUR strengthens, BOTH win<br/>
→ If EUR weakens, BOTH lose<br/>
→ Correlation: HIGH (+0.50 to +0.70)<br/>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Positions:</Highlight>** Long EUR/USD + Short USD/JPY<br/>

→ Common currency: USD<br/>
→ Both are SHORT USD<br/>
→ If USD strengthens, BOTH lose<br/>
→ If USD weakens, BOTH win<br/>
→ Correlation: HIGH (+0.60 to +0.80)<br/>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Positions:</Highlight>** Long EUR/USD + Long GBP/JPY<br/>

→ No common currency<br/>
→ EUR, USD, GBP, JPY all different<br/>
→ Correlation: LOW to MODERATE<br/>

### <Highlight variant='accent'>METHOD 2: THE VISUAL OVERLAY TEST</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Step 1:</Highlight>** Open your charting platform

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Step 2:</Highlight>** Overlay the two instruments you're considering

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Step 3:</Highlight>** Observe:

→ Do they move in the same direction most of the time? → High positive correlation<br/>
→ Do they move in opposite directions most of the time? → High negative correlation<br/>
→ Do they move independently? → Low correlation

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Example:</Highlight>**

EUR/USD (blue line) overlaid with GBP/USD (red line):

→ Both lines rise and fall together<br/>
→ When EUR/USD spikes up, GBP/USD usually spikes up<br/>
→ When EUR/USD drops, GBP/USD usually drops<br/>
→ Conclusion: High correlation (+0.80+)

### <Highlight variant='accent'>METHOD 3: THE CORRELATION COEFFICIENT CALCULATOR</Highlight>

Use a statistical tool (Excel, TradingView, Python) to calculate correlation:

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Excel formula:</Highlight>**

<Math>{'\\text{CORREL(Range1, Range2)}'}</Math>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Example:</Highlight>**

→ Range1: Last 100 daily closes of EUR/USD<br/>
→ Range2: Last 100 daily closes of GBP/USD<br/>
→ Result: +0.87<br/>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Interpretation:</Highlight>** Very high positive correlation

  <AlertDescription>
    When positions share correlation, your effective risk exceeds the sum of
    individual risks.
    <br />

    <br />

    <br />
    <br />

    <br />

    <br />

    <br />
    <br />

    <br />
    3 positions at 2% each → Simple heat = 6% / Average correlation = +0.80
    <br />
    Effective Heat = 6% × (1 + 0.80 / 2) = 6% × 1.40 = 8.4%
    <br />
    <br />
    <strong>Example 3 — The EUR trap:</strong>
    <br />
    5 EUR-based pairs at 2% each → Simple heat = 10% / Average correlation = +0.90
    <br />

    <br />

## BASIC HEDGING TECHNIQUES

Now let's discuss how to actively reduce portfolio heat and correlation risk through hedging.

**<Highlight variant='blue'>HEDGING = Taking offsetting positions to reduce overall exposure</Highlight>**

### <Highlight variant='accent'>HEDGE TYPE 1: DIRECT HEDGE (FOREX)</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Situation:</Highlight>**

→ You're long EUR/USD (1% risk)<br/>
→ News event approaching, high volatility expected<br/>
→ You want to protect the position but not close it<br/>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Direct hedge:</Highlight>**

→ Open a SHORT EUR/USD position (0.5% risk)<br/>
→ Net exposure: 0.5% long EUR/USD<br/>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Outcome scenarios:</Highlight>**

**If EUR/USD rises:**

→ Long position: +2% (1:2 RR)<br/>
→ Short hedge: -0.5%<br/>
→ Net: +1.5% ✅

**If EUR/USD falls:**

→ Long position: -1%<br/>
→ Short hedge: +1% (if it hits 1:2 target)<br/>
→ Net: 0% to +0.5% ✅

\*\*<Highlight variant='blue'>Purpose:</Highlight> Reduces risk during uncertain events while maintaining position.

### <Highlight variant='accent'>HEDGE TYPE 2: CORRELATED HEDGE (STOCKS)</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Situation:</Highlight>**

→ You're long AAPL, MSFT, GOOGL (tech stocks, 3% total risk)<br/>
→ Tech sector looking vulnerable<br/>
→ Don't want to close positions (still like individual setups)<br/>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Correlated hedge:</Highlight>**

→ Short QQQ (NASDAQ ETF) at 1% risk<br/>
→ Net tech exposure: ~2% long (reduced from 3%)

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Outcome:</Highlight>**

**If tech sector rallies:**

→ AAPL, MSFT, GOOGL: +6% combined<br/>
→ QQQ short: -1%<br/>
→ Net: +5%

**If tech sector falls:**

→ AAPL, MSFT, GOOGL: -3% combined<br/>
→ QQQ short: +2%<br/>
→ Net: -1% (protected from -3%)

### <Highlight variant='accent'>HEDGE TYPE 3: INVERSE CORRELATION HEDGE</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Situation:</Highlight>**

→ You're long stocks (risk-on positions)<br/>
→ Market volatility increasing<br/>
→ Want protection without closing positions<br/>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Inverse hedge:</Highlight>**

→ Long Gold (0.5-1% risk)<br/>
→ Long VIX (volatility index) via options<br/>
→ Long USD (via UUP or DXY futures)<br/>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Purpose:</Highlight>**

→ Gold typically rises when stocks fall<br/>
→ VIX spikes when stocks crash<br/>
→ USD strengthens during flight-to-safety<br/>

**<Highlight variant='yellow'>This is portfolio insurance, not a perfect hedge.</Highlight>**

### <Highlight variant='accent'>HEDGE TYPE 4: OPTIONS HEDGING (ADVANCED)</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Situation:</Highlight>**

→ You're long 100 shares of AAPL at $180 (risk $400 if stop at $176 hit)<br/>
→ Earnings report tonight (high volatility)<br/>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Options hedge:</Highlight>**

→ Buy 1 PUT option at $175 strike (cost $200)<br/>
→ Maximum loss now: $500 + $200 = $700 (but you have time to exit before $175)<br/>

**<Highlight variant='blue'>Outcome:</Highlight>**

**If AAPL rallies to $200:**

→ Stock gain: $2,000<br/>
→ PUT expires worthless: -$200<br/>
→ Net: +$1,800<br/>

**If AAPL crashes to $160:**

→ Stock loss: -$2,000<br/>
→ PUT gain: +$1,500 (ITM)<br/>
→ Net: -$500 (much better than -$2,000)<br/>

**<Highlight variant='yellow'>Note:</Highlight>** Options hedging is complex. Don't attempt without education.
