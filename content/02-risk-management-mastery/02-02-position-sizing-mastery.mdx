## The Position Sizing Hierarchy

Before we dive into specific methods, you need to understand the <Highlight variant="yellow">hierarchy of position sizing decisions</Highlight>.
Think of this as building a <Highlight variant="blue">fortress layer by layer</Highlight>:

### <Highlight variant="accent">Layer 1: Account Risk Tolerance (The Bedrock)</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Question:</Highlight>** How much of my total account am I willing to risk on a single trade?

This is your starting point. Everything else flows from this decision.

**Professional standard:** <Highlight variant="blue">1-2% per trade</Highlight><br/>
**Conservative:** <Highlight variant="blue">0.5-1% per trade</Highlight><br/>
**Aggressive (dangerous):** <Highlight variant="blue">3-5% per trade</Highlight><br/>
**Suicidal:** <Highlight variant="blue">5%+ per trade</Highlight><br/>

### <Highlight variant="accent">Layer 2: Setup Quality Assessment (The Foundation Stones)</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Question:</Highlight>** Does this specific setup deserve my full risk allocation, or less?

Not all setups are <Highlight variant="blue">created equal</Highlight>. A <Highlight variant="green">perfect A+</Highlight> setup might get your <Highlight variant="green">full 2%</Highlight>.
A <Highlight variant="yellow">questionable C-grade</Highlight> setup might only get <Highlight variant="yellow">0.5%</Highlight>.

### <Highlight variant="accent">Layer 3: Market Volatility Adjustment (The Adaptive Layer)</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Question:</Highlight>** How volatile is this market right now, and how should that affect my size?

In calm markets, you can trade normal size. In <Highlight variant="red">highly volatile</Highlight> markets, you <Highlight variant="yellow">reduce size</Highlight> to account
for the increased uncertainty.

### <Highlight variant="accent">Layer 4: Technical Stop Placement (The Execution Layer)</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Question:</Highlight>** Where does my stop-loss need to be based on market structure?

Your stop placement is determined by technical analysis—support/resistance, chart patterns, volatility.
Your <Highlight variant="yellow">position size</Highlight> is then <Highlight variant="yellow">calculated backward</Highlight> from your stop distance.

### <Highlight variant="accent">Layer 5: Leverage and Margin Considerations (The Reality Check)</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Question:</Highlight>** Can I actually execute this position size given my broker's margin requirements?

Finally, you verify that your intended position size is executable within your margin constraints.

**This is the correct sequence.** Most amateur traders do it backward — they pick a position size
based on <Highlight variant="yellow">"what feels right,"</Highlight> then slap on a <Highlight variant="red">random stop-loss</Highlight>. That's building your fortress in quicksand.

## THE FOUNDATION: FIXED FRACTIONAL POSITION SIZING

Let's start with the bedrock — the non-negotiable baseline that every trader must master:

### <Highlight variant="accent">Fixed Fractional Position Sizing</Highlight>

This is beautifully simple:

**You risk the same percentage of your current capital on every trade.**

Not the same dollar amount. Not a random amount based on how you feel. <Highlight variant="blue">The same percentage.</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="yellow">The Formula</Highlight>**

<Math>
  {
    '\\text{Position Size} = \\frac{\\text{Account Size} \\times \\text{Risk \\%}}{\\text{Entry Price} - \\text{Stop Loss Price}}'
  }
</Math>
<br />

Or more universally:

<Math>
  {
    '\\text{Position Size} = \\frac{\\text{Account Risk in \\$}}{\\text{Trade Risk in \\$}}'
  }
</Math>
<br />

Let's break this down with a concrete example:

### <Highlight variant="accent">EXAMPLE 1: FOREX TRADE (EUR/USD)</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Your situation:</Highlight>**

→ Account size: <Highlight variant="blue">$10,000</Highlight><br/>
→ Risk per trade: 1% = <Highlight variant="blue">$100</Highlight><br/>
→ Entry price: <Highlight variant="blue">1.1000</Highlight><br/>
→ Stop loss: <Highlight variant="blue">1.0950</Highlight><br/>
→ Trade risk: <Highlight variant="blue">50 pips (0.0050)</Highlight><br/>

**For forex, position size is calculated in lots:**<br/>

<Math>
  {
    '\\text{Position Size} = \\frac{\\text{Risk in \\$}}{\\text{Pips at Risk} \\times \\text{Pip Value}}'
  }
</Math>

For EUR/USD, 1 pip = $10 per standard lot (100,000 units)

<Math>
  {'\\begin{aligned}' +
    '\\text{Position Size} &= \\frac{\\$100}{50 \\text{ pips} \\times \\$10 \\text{ per lot}} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Position Size} &= \\frac{\\$100}{\\$500 \\text{ per lot}} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Position Size} &= 0.2 \\text{ standard lots} = 2 \\text{ mini lots} = 20 \\text{ micro lots}' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>

**<Highlight variant="yellow">Translation:</Highlight>** You'd trade 20,000 units of EUR/USD

If your stop is hit, you lose exactly $100 (1% of your account).
If you hit a 1:2 target at 1.1100 (100 pips), you make $200 (2% of your account).

**This is the baseline. This is your shield.**

### <Highlight variant="accent">EXAMPLE 2: STOCK TRADE (AAPL)</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Your situation:</Highlight>**

→ Account size: <Highlight variant="blue">$50,000</Highlight><br/>
→ Risk per trade: 1% = <Highlight variant="blue">$500</Highlight><br/>
→ Entry price: <Highlight variant="blue">$180.00</Highlight><br/>
→ Stop loss: <Highlight variant="blue">$176.00</Highlight><br/>
→ Trade risk: <Highlight variant="blue">$4.00</Highlight> per share<br/>

<Math>
  {'\\text{Position Size} = \\frac{\\$500}{\\$4.00} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Position Size} = 125 \\text{ shares}'}
</Math>

**<Highlight variant="yellow">Translation:</Highlight>** You'd buy 125 shares of AAPL

If your stop is hit, you lose exactly $500 (1% of your account).
If you hit a 1:2 target at $188.00 (+$8), you make $1,000 (2% of your account).

### <Highlight variant="accent">EXAMPLE 3: CRYPTO TRADE (BTC/USD)</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Your situation:</Highlight>**

→ Account size: <Highlight variant="blue">$20,000</Highlight><br/>
→ Risk per trade: 1.5% = <Highlight variant="blue">$300</Highlight><br/>
→ Entry price: <Highlight variant="blue">$45,000</Highlight><br/>
→ Stop loss: <Highlight variant="blue">$44,000</Highlight><br/>
→ Trade risk: <Highlight variant="blue">$1,000</Highlight> per BTC<br/>

<Math>
  {'\\text{Position Size} = \\frac{\\$300}{\\$1,000} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Position Size} = 0.3 \\text{ BTC}'}
</Math>

**<Highlight variant="yellow">Translation:</Highlight>** You'd buy 0.3 Bitcoin

If your stop is hit, you lose exactly $300 (1.5% of your account).
If you hit a 1:2 target at $47,000 (+$2,000 per BTC), you make $600 (3% of your account).

## ADVANCED METHOD #1: ATR-BASED VOLATILITY SIZING

Fixed fractional sizing (1-2% risk) is your <Highlight variant="blue">baseline</Highlight>.
But <Highlight variant="yellow">professional traders</Highlight> add a <Highlight variant="yellow">crucial layer of sophistication</Highlight>:

**<Highlight variant="red">Adjusting position size based on market volatility.</Highlight>**

Why? Because not all markets and not all trades have the same <Highlight variant="yellow">volatility characteristics</Highlight>.

<Highlight variant="blue">Consider:</Highlight>

→ Low volatility: EUR/USD during London session (average 50 pip range)<br/>
→ High volatility: GBP/JPY during news event (average 200 pip range)<br/>

If you place your stops at the same technical levels <Highlight variant="red">without adjusting for volatility</Highlight>, you'll either:

1\. <Highlight variant="yellow">Get stopped out prematurely in high volatility</Highlight> (noise takes you out)<br/>
2\. <Highlight variant="yellow">Risk too much in low volatility</Highlight> (tight stop means huge position)

**ATR (Average True Range) solves this.**

### <Highlight variant="accent">WHAT IS ATR?</Highlight>

ATR measures the <Highlight variant="yellow">average range of price movement</Highlight> over a <Highlight variant="blue">specified period</Highlight> (typically 14 periods).

→ <Highlight variant="yellow">High ATR = High volatility</Highlight> = Need wider stops =<Highlight variant="red"> Smaller position size</Highlight><br/>
→ <Highlight variant="yellow">Low ATR = Low volatility</Highlight> = Can use tighter stops =<Highlight variant="green"> Larger position size</Highlight>

### <Highlight variant="accent">THE ATR POSITION SIZING FORMULA</Highlight>

<Math>
  {'\\begin{aligned}' +
    '\\text{Stop Distance} &= \\text{Entry Price} \\pm (\\text{ATR} \\times \\text{Multiplier}) \\\\' +
    '\\text{Position Size} &= \\frac{\\text{Account Risk in }\\$}{\\text{Stop Distance in }\\$}' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>
<br />

**<Highlight variant="blue">Typical ATR multipliers:</Highlight>**

→ <Highlight variant="yellow">2× ATR</Highlight>: Aggressive (more likely to get stopped out by noise)<br/>
→ <Highlight variant="yellow">3× ATR</Highlight>: Balanced (professional standard)<br/>
→ <Highlight variant="yellow">4× ATR</Highlight>: Conservative (gives trade more room)<br/>

### <Highlight variant="accent">EXAMPLE: ATR-BASED SIZING ON EUR/USD</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Your situation:</Highlight>**

→ Account: <Highlight variant="blue">$10,000</Highlight><br/>
→ Risk per trade: 1% = <Highlight variant="blue">$100</Highlight><br/>
→ Entry: <Highlight variant="blue">1.1000</Highlight><br/>
→ 14-period ATR: <Highlight variant="blue">0.0060</Highlight> (60 pips)<br/>
→ Multiplier: <Highlight variant="yellow">3× ATR</Highlight><br/>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Calculate stop distance:</Highlight>**

<Math>
  {'\\begin{aligned}' +
    '\\text{Stop Distance} &= 3 \\times 60 \\text{ pips} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Stop Distance} &= 180 \\text{ pips} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Stop Loss} &= 1.1000 - 0.0180 \\\\' +
    '\\text{Stop Loss} &= 1.0820' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Calculate position size:</Highlight>**

<Math>
  {'\\begin{aligned}' +
    '\\text{Position Size} &= \\frac{\\$100}{180 \\text{ pips} \\times \\$10 \\text{ per lot}} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Position Size} &= \\frac{\\$100}{\\$1,800 \\text{ per lot}} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Position Size} &= 0.0555 \\text{ lots} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Position Size} &= 5.5 \\text{ micro lots} = 5,550 \\text{ units}' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Compare to fixed 50-pip stop:</Highlight>**

<Math>
  {'\\begin{aligned}' +
    '\\text{Position Size} &= \\frac{\\$100}{50 \\text{ pips} \\times \\$10 \\text{ per lot}} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Position Size} &= 0.2 \\text{ lots} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Position Size} &= 20,000 \\text{ units}' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>

**Notice the difference?**

ATR-based sizing gave you a <Highlight variant="yellow">3.6× smaller position</Highlight> because the <Highlight variant="yellow">wider stop</Highlight>
(appropriate for volatility) automatically <Highlight variant="green">reduced your exposure</Highlight>.

**This is beautiful risk management:**

→ You're still risking exactly 1% ($100)<br/>
→ But your stop is intelligently placed based on market volatility<br/>
→ You won't get stopped out by normal market noise<br/>
→ Your position size automatically adjusts to maintain consistent risk

### <Highlight variant="accent">THE ATR ADVANTAGE IN DIFFERENT MARKET CONDITIONS</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Scenario 1: Low Volatility (ATR = 30 pips)</Highlight>**

<Math>
  {'\\begin{aligned}' +
    '\\text{Stop} &= 3 \\times 30 \\text{ pips} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Stop} &= 90 \\text{ pips} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Position Size} &= \\frac{\\$100}{90 \\text{ pips} \\times \\$10 \\text{ per lot}} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Position Size} &= 0.111 \\text{ lots} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Position Size} &= 11,100 \\text{ units}' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Scenario 2: High Volatility (ATR = 120 pips)</Highlight>**

<Math>
  {'\\begin{aligned}' +
    '\\text{Stop} &= 3 \\times 120 \\text{ pips} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Stop} &= 360 \\text{ pips} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Position Size} &= \\frac{\\$100}{360 \\text{ pips} \\times \\$10 \\text{ per lot}} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Position Size} &= 0.0278 \\text{ lots} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Position Size} &= 2,780 \\text{ units}' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>

**See the elegance?**

In <Highlight variant="blue">low volatility</Highlight>, you get a <Highlight variant="yellow">larger position</Highlight> because you can use a tighter stop.<br/>
In <Highlight variant="blue">high volatility</Highlight>, you get a <Highlight variant="yellow">smaller position</Highlight> because you need a wider stop.

**But in <Highlight variant="blue">both cases</Highlight>, you're risking exactly <Highlight variant="yellow">1%</Highlight> of your account.**

Your position size adapts to market conditions automatically. This is professional-grade risk management.

## ADVANCED METHOD #2: THE KELLY CRITERION (USE WITH CAUTION)

Now we enter <Highlight variant="red">dangerous territory</Highlight>.

The <Highlight variant="blue">Kelly Criterion</Highlight> is a mathematical formula developed by <CustomLink href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Larry_Kelly_Jr." target="_blank">John Kelly Jr.</CustomLink> in 1956 to determine <Highlight variant="yellow">optimal</Highlight> bet sizing for favorable odds.

Many professional traders use it. Many more have been destroyed by it.

**The <Highlight variant="blue">Kelly formula:</Highlight>**

<Math>
  {
    '\\text{Kelly \\%} = \\frac{(\\text{Win Rate} \\times \\text{Avg Win}) - (\\text{Loss Rate} \\times \\text{Avg Loss})}{\\text{Avg Win}}'
  }
</Math>

Or simplified for trading:

<Math>
  {
    '\\text{Kelly \\%} = \\text{Win Rate} - \\frac{1 - \\text{Win Rate}}{\\text{Risk-Reward Ratio}}'
  }
</Math>

### <Highlight variant="accent">EXAMPLE: KELLY CALCULATION</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Your system:</Highlight>**

→ Win rate: <Highlight variant="blue">55%</Highlight><br/>
→ Risk-reward ratio: <Highlight variant="blue">1:2</Highlight><br/>
→ Average win: <Highlight variant="blue">$200</Highlight><br/>
→ Average loss: <Highlight variant="blue">$100</Highlight><br/>

<Math>
  {'\\begin{aligned}' +
    '\\text{Kelly \\%} &= 0.55 - \\frac{1 - 0.55}{2} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Kelly \\%} &= 0.55 - 0.225 \\\\' +
    '\\text{Kelly \\%} &= 0.325 = 32.5\\%' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>

**The Kelly Criterion says you should risk <Highlight variant="yellow">32.5%</Highlight> of your capital per trade.**

**<span style={{color: 'red'}}>DO NOT DO THIS.</span>**

### <Highlight variant="accent">WHY FULL KELLY IS SUICIDE</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Full Kelly</Highlight>** <Highlight variant="yellow">maximizes long-term growth rate</Highlight>, but it comes with <Highlight variant="red">catastrophic drawdown risk</Highlight>:

→ Full Kelly (<Highlight variant="yellow">32.5%</Highlight>): Max drawdown can exceed 80%<br/>
→ One bad streak and you're <Highlight variant="red">psychologically obliterated</Highlight><br/>
→ Requires perfect estimation of <Highlight variant="yellow">win rate</Highlight> and <Highlight variant="yellow">RR</Highlight> (impossible in trading)<br/>
→ Assumes <Highlight variant="yellow">infinite number of trials</Highlight> (you don't have infinite capital)<br/>

**Professional traders use <Highlight variant="blue">fractional Kelly</Highlight>:**

→ <Highlight variant="yellow">Half Kelly</Highlight> (<Highlight variant="yellow">16.25%</Highlight>): Still too aggressive; max drawdown ~50%<br/>
→ <Highlight variant="yellow">Quarter Kelly</Highlight> (<Highlight variant="yellow">8.1%</Highlight>): Aggressive but survivable; max drawdown ~25%<br/>
→ <Highlight variant="yellow">One-Eighth Kelly</Highlight> (<Highlight variant="yellow">4%</Highlight>): Conservative; max drawdown ~15%<br/>

**Most professionals use 1/4 to 1/8 Kelly at most.**

### <Highlight variant="accent">PRACTICAL KELLY APPLICATION</Highlight>

Let's redo the calculation with <Highlight variant="yellow">Quarter Kelly</Highlight>:

<Math>
  {'\\begin{aligned}' +
    '\\text{Full Kelly} &= 32.5\\% \\\\' +
    '\\text{Quarter Kelly} &= \\frac{32.5\\%}{4} = 8.1\\%' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>

**Still too high for most traders.**

**Here's the intelligent approach:**

Use <Highlight variant="blue">Kelly</Highlight> as an <Highlight variant="yellow">upper bound</Highlight>, not a <Highlight variant="yellow">target</Highlight>:

1\. <Highlight variant="yellow">Calculate Full Kelly</Highlight><br/>
2\. <Highlight variant="yellow">Divide by 4 to 8</Highlight><br/>
3\. <Highlight variant="yellow">Cap at 2% regardless</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Example:</Highlight>**

→ Full Kelly = <Highlight variant="yellow">32.5%</Highlight><br/>
→ Divide by 8 = <Highlight variant="yellow">4.06%</Highlight><br/>
→ Cap at 2% = Use <Highlight variant="yellow">2%</Highlight><br/>

**You're now using Kelly to confirm that your 1-2% risk is within optimal parameters,
not to encourage reckless sizing.**

### <Highlight variant="accent">KELLY WARNING SIGNS</Highlight>

If your Kelly calculation suggests <Highlight variant="yellow">>50%</Highlight> position sizing:

→ Your <Highlight variant="yellow">win rate estimate</Highlight> is probably wrong<br/>
→ Your <Highlight variant="yellow">risk-reward estimate</Highlight> is probably wrong<br/>
→ You're overfitting to a <Highlight variant="yellow">small sample size</Highlight><br/>
→ **Do <Highlight variant="red">NOT</Highlight> increase position size**<br/>

**Bottom line: Kelly is a <Highlight variant="blue">mathematical curiosity</Highlight> and a <Highlight variant="blue">reality check</Highlight>,

## LEVERAGE, EXPOSURE, AND MARGIN: THE HIDDEN MULTIPLIERS

Now let's address the <Highlight variant="red">silent killer</Highlight> that most traders don't fully understand:

**<Highlight variant="blue">Leverage and margin mechanics.</Highlight>**

### <Highlight variant="accent">WHAT IS LEVERAGE?</Highlight>

Leverage allows you to control a position larger than your account balance.

**<Highlight variant="blue">Examples:</Highlight>**

→ <Highlight variant="yellow">10:1 leverage:</Highlight> <Highlight variant="blue">$1,000</Highlight> controls <Highlight variant="blue">$10,000</Highlight> position<br/>
→ <Highlight variant="yellow">50:1 leverage:</Highlight> <Highlight variant="blue">$1,000</Highlight> controls <Highlight variant="blue">$50,000</Highlight> position<br/>
→ <Highlight variant="yellow">100:1 leverage:</Highlight> <Highlight variant="blue">$1,000</Highlight> controls <Highlight variant="blue">$100,000</Highlight> position<br/>

**Critical distinction:**

**<Highlight variant="red">Available leverage ≠ Used leverage</Highlight>**

Your broker might offer <Highlight variant="yellow">10:1 leverage</Highlight>, but that doesn't mean you should use it.

**<Highlight variant="blue">Used leverage</Highlight> is determined by your position size relative to your account:**

<Math>
  {
    '\\text{Used Leverage} = \\frac{\\text{Total Position Value}}{\\text{Account Equity}}'
  }
</Math>

### <Highlight variant="accent">EXAMPLE: CONTROLLING YOUR ACTUAL LEVERAGE</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Your situation:</Highlight>**

→ Account: <Highlight variant="blue">$10,000</Highlight><br/>
→ Broker offers: <Highlight variant="yellow">100:1 leverage</Highlight><br/>
→ You trade: <Highlight variant="yellow">0.1 standard lots</Highlight> of EUR/USD (10,000 units)<br/>
→ Position value: <Highlight variant="blue">$11,000</Highlight> (if EUR/USD = 1.1000)<br/>
→ Your used leverage:

<Math>
  {
    '\\text{Used Leverage} = \\frac{\\text{Total Position Value}}{\\text{Account Equity}} = \\frac{\\text{11,000}}{\\text{10,000}} = 1.1:1'
  }
</Math>

Even though your broker offers <Highlight variant="yellow">100:1 leverage</Highlight>, you're only using <Highlight variant="yellow">1.1:1</Highlight>.

**This is intelligent leverage use.**

### <Highlight variant="accent">THE LEVERAGE DANGER ZONE</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Safe Zone</Highlight> (1:1 to 5:1 used leverage):**

→ Position value is <Highlight variant="yellow">1-5× your account</Highlight><br/>
→ Allows for normal market movements<br/>
→ Low risk of margin calls<br/>
→ **Recommended for most traders**<br/>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Caution Zone</Highlight> (5:1 to 10:1 used leverage):**

→ Position value is <Highlight variant='yellow'>5-10× your account</Highlight><br/>
→ Requires tight stop losses<br/>
→ Moderate risk of margin calls<br/>
→ **Only for experienced traders with robust systems**<br/>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Danger Zone</Highlight> (10:1 to 30:1 used leverage):**

→ Position value is <Highlight variant="yellow">10-30× your account</Highlight><br/>
→ Very high risk of margin calls<br/>
→ Small adverse moves can wipe account<br/>
→ **Only for professionals with exceptional risk management**<br/>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Suicide Zone</Highlight> (30:1+ used leverage):**

→ Position value exceeds <Highlight variant="yellow">30× your account</Highlight><br/>
→ Almost certain destruction<br/>
→ **<Highlight variant="red">Avoid at all costs</Highlight>**<br/>

### <Highlight variant="accent">MARGIN CALL MATHEMATICS</Highlight>

Your broker requires **margin** — a percentage of your position value held as <Highlight variant="yellow">collateral</Highlight>.

<Math>
  {
    '\\text{Margin Requirement} = \\frac{\\text{Position Value}}{\\text{Leverage}}'
  }
</Math>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Example:</Highlight>**

→ Position: <Highlight variant="blue">$50,000</Highlight><br/>
→ Leverage: <Highlight variant="yellow">50:1</Highlight><br/>
→ Margin required: <Highlight variant="blue">$50,000 / 50 = $1,000</Highlight><br/>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Margin call trigger:</Highlight>**

Most brokers issue margin calls when your equity falls to <Highlight variant="yellow">100-50%</Highlight> of required margin.

**If you use high leverage, a small adverse move triggers margin call:**

→ Account: <Highlight variant="blue">$2,000</Highlight><br/>
→ Position: <Highlight variant="blue">$100,000</Highlight> (50:1 leverage)<br/>
→ Margin required: <Highlight variant="blue">$2,000</Highlight><br/>
→ Available for losses: ~<Highlight variant="blue">$0-$1,000</Highlight><br/>

**A 1% adverse move = <Highlight variant="blue">$1,000</Highlight> loss = margin call.**

**<Highlight variant="yellow">Compare to low leverage:</Highlight>**

→ Account: <Highlight variant="blue">$10,000</Highlight><br/>
→ Position: <Highlight variant="blue">$20,000</Highlight> (2:1 leverage)<br/>
→ Margin required: <Highlight variant="blue">$400</Highlight> (at 50:1 available)<br/>
→ Available for losses: ~<Highlight variant="blue">$9,600</Highlight><br/>

**A 1% adverse move = <Highlight variant="blue">$200</Highlight> loss = barely noticeable.**

**<Highlight variant="blue">The lesson:</Highlight> Control your actual position size, not just your percentage risk.**

## PORTFOLIO HEAT: THE FORGOTTEN RISK

Position sizing isn't just about individual trades — it's about <Highlight variant="yellow">total exposure</Highlight>.

**<Highlight variant="blue">Portfolio heat</Highlight> = Total capital at risk across <Highlight variant="yellow">all open positions</Highlight>**

### <Highlight variant="accent">THE PORTFOLIO HEAT RULE</Highlight>

**Never have more than 6-10% of your total capital at risk simultaneously.**

**<Highlight variant="blue">Example:</Highlight>**

You have a <Highlight variant="blue">$20,000</Highlight> account and follow <Highlight variant="yellow">1% risk per trade</Highlight>:

**Maximum simultaneous positions:**

→ 8% max portfolio heat<br/>
→ Risk per trade: 1% = <Highlight variant="blue">$200</Highlight><br/>
→ Maximum positions: <Highlight variant="yellow">8 trades</Highlight><br/>

**If you have 10 positions open, each risking <Highlight variant="blue">$200</Highlight>:**

→ Total portfolio heat: <Highlight variant="blue">$2,000</Highlight> = **10% of account**<br/>
→ You've hit your limit — **<Highlight variant="red">do not take another trade</Highlight>**<br/>

### <Highlight variant="accent">WHY PORTFOLIO HEAT MATTERS</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Scenario:</Highlight> The Correlation Trap**

You think you're diversified:

→ Long EUR/USD<br/>
→ Long GBP/USD<br/>
→ Long AUD/USD<br/>
→ Long NZD/USD<br/>
→ Long EUR/GBP<br/>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Reality:</Highlight> All five are USD shorts.**

If USD strengthens, **all five positions lose simultaneously.**

What you thought was 5× 1% risk (5% total) is actually more like **1 position with 5% risk**.

**During the 2016 Trump election surprise, USD spiked. Traders with multiple USD shorts
got obliterated in minutes.**

### <Highlight variant="accent">MANAGING PORTFOLIO HEAT</Highlight>

→ Track total risk daily<br/>
→ Limit correlated positions (no more than 2-3 in same direction)<br/>
→ Reduce position size when portfolio heat >6%<br/>
→ Close or reduce positions before adding new ones<br/>
→ Use a position tracker (Excel, TradingView, journal)<br/>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Professional approach:</Highlight>**

If you want to add a 9th position but you're at <Highlight variant="yellow">8% portfolio heat</Highlight>:

→ <Highlight variant="yellow">Option A:</Highlight> Reduce size on all positions to <Highlight variant="yellow">0.9% each</Highlight> (allows 9 positions at <Highlight variant="yellow">8.1% heat</Highlight>)<br/>
→ <Highlight variant="yellow">Option B:</Highlight> Close or reduce your weakest position<br/>
→ <Highlight variant="yellow">Option C:</Highlight> Don't take the new trade<br/>

**Discipline over opportunity.**

    '\\text{Position Size} &= \\frac{\\text{Risk in }\\$}{\\text{Trade Risk}}' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>

**<Highlight variant="blue">For forex:</Highlight>**

<Math>
  {
    '\\text{Position Size (lots)} = \\frac{\\text{Risk in }\\$}{\\text{Pips at Risk} \\times \\text{Pip Value per Lot}}'
  }
</Math>

### <Highlight variant="accent">ADVANCED CALCULATOR WITH AMATEUR</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Additional inputs:</Highlight>**

→ ATR value<br/>
→ ATR multiplier<br/>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Formula:</Highlight>**

<Math>
  {'\\begin{aligned}' +
    '\\text{Stop Distance} &= \\text{ATR} \\times \\text{Multiplier} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Stop Loss} &= \\text{Entry} \\pm \\text{Stop Distance} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Position Size} &= \\frac{\\text{Risk in }\\$}{\\text{Stop Distance in }\\$}' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>

### <Highlight variant="accent">PROFESSIONAL CALCULATOR WITH RISK SCORING</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Additional inputs:</Highlight>**

→ Setup Grade (A/B/C)<br/>
→ Score multiplier (1.0 / 0.6 / 0.3)<br/>

**<Highlight variant="blue">Formula:</Highlight>**

<Math>
  {'\\begin{aligned}' +
    '\\text{Adjusted Risk} &= \\text{Base Risk \\%} \\times \\text{Score Multiplier} \\\\' +
    '\\text{Position Size} &= \\frac{\\text{Account Size} \\times \\text{Adjusted Risk}}{\\text{Trade Risk}}' +
    '\\end{aligned}'}
</Math>

## COMMON POSITION SIZING MISTAKES (AND HOW TO AVOID THEM)

### <Highlight variant="accent">MISTAKE #1: Sizing based on desired profit</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="red">Wrong:</Highlight>** "I want to make $500, so I'll calculate position size backward from my target."

**<Highlight variant="green">Right:</Highlight>** "I'll risk 1% ($100), place my stop at the technical level, and let the position size fall where it may."

**Your desired profit is irrelevant. Your risk management is paramount.**

### <Highlight variant="accent">MISTAKE #2: Using the same position size for all trades</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="red">Wrong:</Highlight>** "I always trade 0.5 lots regardless of stop distance."

**<Highlight variant="green">Right:</Highlight>** "My position size adjusts based on my stop loss distance to maintain consistent risk percentage."

**Fixed lot size = variable risk = eventual destruction.**

### <Highlight variant="accent">MISTAKE #3: Increasing size after wins, decreasing after losses</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="red">Wrong:</Highlight>** "I just won three trades, I'm on fire—let's risk 5% on this one!"

**<Highlight variant="green">Right:</Highlight>** "My position size is determined by my system, not my recent results."

**This is the fastest way to give back all profits and more.**

### <Highlight variant="accent">MISTAKE #4: Risking more on "sure things"</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="red">Wrong:</Highlight>** "This setup is perfect — I'm risking 5% because it can't lose."

**<Highlight variant="green">Right:</Highlight>** "No trade is certain. I risk my predetermined percentage regardless of confidence."

**The trades you're most confident in are often the ones that hurt the most.**

### <Highlight variant="accent">MISTAKE #5: Ignoring portfolio heat</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="red">Wrong:</Highlight>** "I have five positions each risking 2%, that's fine — it's only 2% per trade."

**<Highlight variant="green">Right:</Highlight>** "I have 10% portfolio heat, which is my limit. I cannot take another position until one closes."

**Simultaneous losses destroy accounts.**
`

### <Highlight variant="accent">MISTAKE #6: Not adjusting for account growth</Highlight>

**<Highlight variant="red">Wrong:</Highlight>** "My account doubled, but I still risk the same dollar amount."

**<Highlight variant="green">Right:</Highlight>** "My risk percentage stays constant, which means my dollar risk grows with my account."

**Compounding requires adjusting absolute risk as your account grows.**

## THE PSYCHOLOGICAL POWER OF PROPER POSITION SIZING

Beyond the mathematics, proper position sizing has profound psychological benefits:

### <Highlight variant="accent">BENEFIT #1: REMOVES FEAR</Highlight>

When you risk 1%, losing doesn't hurt. You can take your stop without hesitation.

When you risk 10%, every tick against you is torture. You'll move your stop or hold hoping for a reversal.

**Small position size = clear thinking.**

### <Highlight variant="accent">BENEFIT #2: ENABLES DISCIPLINE</Highlight>

With proper sizing, you can follow your system mechanically without emotional interference.

With oversizing, every decision becomes emotional—should I hold? Should I exit early? Should I add to this loser?

**Proper sizing = robotic execution.**

### <Highlight variant="accent">BENEFIT #3: BUILDS CONFIDENCE</Highlight>

A 10-trade losing streak at 1% risk is annoying but manageable.

A 3-trade losing streak at 10% risk is psychologically devastating.

**Surviving losses builds confidence. Proper sizing makes survival guaranteed.**

### <Highlight variant="accent">BENEFIT #4: ALLOWS PATIENCE</Highlight>

When your positions are sized properly, you can let winners run without anxiety.

When oversized, every move against you feels catastrophic, causing premature exits.

**Small risk = big patience.**
